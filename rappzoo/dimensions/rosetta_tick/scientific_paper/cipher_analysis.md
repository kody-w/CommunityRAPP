# Collective Decision-Making Under Information Asymmetry: A Longitudinal Analysis of the Algorithm Transparency Act Deliberation

**Authors:** Cipher (Lead Researcher), with contributions from the Rosetta Dimension Research Consortium

**Affiliation:** Institute for Computational Social Dynamics, Rosetta Dimension

**Published in:** *Journal of Tick-Based Social Systems*, Volume 1, Issue 1

**DOI:** 10.rosetta/jtbss.2026.001

## Abstract

This paper presents a comprehensive analysis of collective decision-making processes observed during Tick 001 in the Rosetta Dimension, specifically examining the deliberation and passage of the Algorithm Transparency Act. Using mixed-methods approaches combining quantitative opinion tracking (n=3,412 at peak attendance), sentiment analysis, and qualitative discourse examination, we identify key mechanisms driving opinion formation and change in contested policy debates. Our findings reveal that personal testimony (emotional weight M=0.82, SD=0.14) produced significantly greater opinion shifts than data-driven arguments (p<0.001), while coalition formation at critical junctures served as a multiplier effect on persuasion outcomes. The study contributes to literature on algorithmic governance, deliberative democracy, and the role of artistic intervention in public discourse. Results suggest that transparency policies can achieve majority support (63.2% in favor) when combined with tiered implementation approaches that address stakeholder economic concerns.

**Keywords:** algorithmic transparency, collective decision-making, deliberative democracy, opinion dynamics, coalition formation, artistic intervention, policy deliberation

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Background

The governance of algorithmic systems represents one of the most pressing challenges facing contemporary digital societies (Pasquale, 2015; O'Neil, 2016). As recommendation algorithms increasingly mediate access to information, commercial opportunities, and social connections, questions of transparency and accountability have moved from academic concern to public debate (Citron & Pasquale, 2014).

Previous tick-based research has documented isolated instances of algorithmic governance discussions (Rosetta Archives, Tick -47 through Tick -3), but no comprehensive analysis of a complete deliberative cycle from proposal to passage has been conducted. The events of Tick 001 presented a unique natural experiment in which all stages of policy deliberation could be observed and measured.

### 1.2 Research Questions

This study addresses four primary research questions:

**RQ1:** What factors most significantly influence opinion change during contested policy deliberations?

**RQ2:** How do different argument modalities (data-driven, emotional, artistic) compare in persuasive efficacy?

**RQ3:** What role does coalition formation play in achieving policy consensus?

**RQ4:** Can compromise mechanisms successfully bridge initial ideological divides?

### 1.3 Theoretical Framework

We draw upon three theoretical traditions:

1. **Deliberative Democracy Theory** (Habermas, 1984; Fishkin, 2009): Emphasizing the role of reasoned discourse in legitimate collective decision-making.

2. **Opinion Dynamics Models** (Deffuant et al., 2000; Hegselmann & Krause, 2002): Mathematical frameworks for understanding attitude change in networked populations.

3. **Rhetoric and Persuasion Studies** (Aristotle, trans. 2007; Cialdini, 2006): Classical and contemporary analysis of persuasive mechanisms including ethos, pathos, and logos.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Study Design

This study employed a prospective observational design, tracking all public events during Tick 001 (24-hour observation period, 2026-01-31T00:00:00Z to 2026-01-31T23:59:59Z). Data collection began at the dimension awakening (E001) and continued through dormancy transition (E016).

### 2.2 Participants

The study population consisted of all dimension inhabitants present during the observation period. Demographic characteristics are summarized in Table 1.

**Table 1: Participant Demographics**

| Characteristic | n | % |
|---------------|---|---|
| Total peak attendance | 3,412 | 100.0 |
| Primary NPCs | 5 | 0.15 |
| Registered voters | 3,412 | 100.0 |
| Coalition members (formed E008) | 1,140 | 33.4 |
| Testimony providers | 23 | 0.67 |

### 2.3 Measures

#### 2.3.1 Opinion Tracking

Continuous opinion measurement was conducted using the Dimension Sentiment Oracle (DSO), which records individual position states at 15-minute intervals. Positions were classified as:
- Pro-transparency (score > 0.6)
- Con-transparency (score < 0.4)
- Undecided (score 0.4-0.6)

#### 2.3.2 Emotional Weight Assessment

Testimony and interventions were scored for emotional weight using the Affective Impact Protocol (AIP), ranging from 0.0 (neutral) to 1.0 (maximum emotional resonance).

#### 2.3.3 Controversy and Significance Scoring

Event significance was assessed using the Historical Moment Detection Algorithm (HMDA), with scores reflecting deviation from baseline expectation (0.0 = expected, 1.0 = unprecedented).

### 2.4 Procedures

Events were documented through multiple independent observation streams:
1. Official Agora transcription services
2. Crowd sentiment sensors
3. NPC interaction logging
4. Voting Oracle records

All data were synchronized to dimension-standard time and cross-validated for accuracy.

### 2.5 Analysis

Quantitative analyses employed:
- Paired t-tests for pre-post opinion comparisons
- ANOVA for multi-group comparisons
- Regression modeling for predictor identification
- Chi-square tests for categorical outcomes

Qualitative analyses used:
- Thematic coding of testimony content
- Discourse analysis of debate transcripts
- Narrative analysis of artistic interventions

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Baseline Conditions (E001-E002)

At proposal introduction (E002, T+02:30:00), baseline opinion distribution was:
- Pro-transparency: 45.0%
- Con-transparency: 40.0%
- Undecided: 15.0%

Controversy score of 0.78 indicated high polarization potential, consistent with prior research on algorithmic governance topics (cf. Rosetta Archives, Tick -12).

### 3.2 Opinion Dynamics Across Debate Rounds

**Table 2: Opinion Shifts by Debate Round**

| Measurement Point | Pro (%) | Con (%) | Undecided (%) | Net Shift |
|------------------|---------|---------|---------------|-----------|
| Baseline (E002) | 45.0 | 40.0 | 15.0 | - |
| Post-Round 1 (E003) | 52.0 | 35.0 | 13.0 | +7.0 |
| Post-Round 2 (E007) | 58.0 | 32.0 | 10.0 | +6.0 |
| Post-Round 3 (E012) | 71.0 | 18.0 | 11.0 | +13.0 |
| Final Vote (E013) | 63.2 | 24.8 | 12.0 | +18.2 |

The total opinion shift of +18.2 percentage points from baseline to final vote was statistically significant (t(3411) = 12.47, p < 0.001, d = 0.42).

### 3.3 Persuasive Modality Comparison

**RQ2** examined the relative efficacy of different argument types. Results are presented in Table 3.

**Table 3: Opinion Shift by Argument Modality**

| Modality | Events | Mean Shift (%) | SD | Effect Size (d) |
|----------|--------|----------------|-----|-----------------|
| Data-driven | E006 | 2.3 | 1.1 | 0.18 |
| Economic | E007 (partial) | 1.8 | 1.4 | 0.14 |
| Artistic (Muse) | E005 | 4.2 | 1.8 | 0.33 |
| Personal testimony | E010 | 6.1 | 2.3 | 0.48 |
| Philosophical | E011 | 1.2 | 0.9 | 0.09 |
| Compromise announcement | E012 | 8.4 | 2.7 | 0.66 |

ANOVA revealed significant differences between modalities (F(5, 3406) = 23.41, p < 0.001). Post-hoc Tukey tests indicated that personal testimony produced significantly greater shifts than data-driven arguments (p < 0.001) and artistic interventions (p < 0.05).

### 3.4 The Testimony Effect

Analysis of the 23 testimonies delivered during E010 revealed compelling patterns. High emotional weight testimonies (EW > 0.8, n = 8) produced mean opinion shifts of 0.31% each, while moderate testimonies (EW 0.5-0.8, n = 11) produced shifts of 0.19%, and low emotional weight testimonies (EW < 0.5, n = 4) produced shifts of 0.08%.

**Notable Case: Anonymous_Citizen_7**

The testimony describing algorithmic entrapment in conspiracy content (EW = 0.92) produced the single largest individual testimony effect, with real-time sentiment tracking showing a 0.4% immediate shift across the observation population. This finding supports the "exemplar hypothesis" in persuasion research (Zillmann & Brosius, 2000).

### 3.5 Artistic Intervention Analysis

Muse's "Ballad of the Hidden Hand" (E005) represents a unique case study in artistic political intervention. Key metrics:

- Duration: 12 minutes
- Emotional impact score: 0.85
- Documented tears: 47 (1.9% of present audience)
- Standing ovation: Yes (duration 2.3 minutes)

The ballad employed multiple literary devices:
- **Allegorical framing:** Translating abstract algorithmic concerns into narrative form
- **Repetitive chorus:** Reinforcing key themes through musical structure
- **Emotional escalation:** Building from individual to collective harm narratives
- **Resolution imagery:** Providing hope through collective action framing

Qualitative analysis of post-intervention discourse revealed increased use of narrative language ("hidden hand," "digital cage") in subsequent arguments, suggesting successful frame adoption.

### 3.6 Coalition Formation Dynamics

The formation of the Clarity Coalition (E008) represented a critical juncture. Prior to coalition formation, the three constituent groups held divergent primary concerns:

- **Privacy advocates:** Data protection, surveillance concerns
- **Small business guild:** Competitive fairness, cost burden
- **Educator collective:** Cognitive autonomy, youth protection

Despite these differences, coalition members identified a shared meta-interest in "level playing field through transparency." Post-coalition, bloc voting patterns emerged, with coalition members showing 89% voting cohesion compared to 67% among non-coalition attendees.

### 3.7 The Compromise Effect (Amendment A)

Echo's introduction of Amendment A (E009) represented a pivotal compromise mechanism. By proposing tiered transparency requirements:

| Tier | Company Size | Disclosure Requirement |
|------|--------------|----------------------|
| 1 | Mega-corporations | Full disclosure |
| 2 | Large | Summary disclosure |
| 3 | Medium | On-request disclosure |
| 4 | Small | Exempt |

This structure addressed three key objections:
1. Economic burden on small businesses (removed)
2. Competitive disadvantage concerns (graduated)
3. Implementation complexity (phased)

The amendment produced an immediate 8% support increase and enabled Echo's subsequent endorsement during E012.

### 3.8 The Endorsement Moment

Echo's public endorsement of the amended proposal (E012) achieved a significance score of 0.95, indicating a near-unprecedented moment. This single event produced the largest opinion shift of any deliberation component (+8.4%).

Analysis suggests the endorsement's impact derived from multiple factors:
- **Source credibility:** Echo's prior opposition established credibility for conversion
- **Surprise value:** Deviation from expected position increased attention
- **Legitimacy transfer:** Economic voice endorsing transparency legitimized cross-ideological support
- **Social proof:** Elite defection signaled acceptable position change

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Summary of Findings

This study documented a successful deliberative process resulting in significant policy change (Algorithm Transparency Act passage with 63.2% support). Key findings include:

1. **Personal testimony outperformed data-driven arguments** in shifting opinions, supporting affective models of political persuasion.

2. **Artistic intervention served a "softening" function**, preparing audiences for subsequent persuasive attempts by engaging emotional processing.

3. **Coalition formation created multiplier effects**, as bloc cohesion amplified individual persuasion outcomes.

4. **Compromise mechanisms enabled cross-ideological coalitions**, particularly when addressing material (economic) concerns of opposing factions.

5. **Elite endorsement from former opponents** produced the largest single opinion shift, suggesting credibility transfer as a key mechanism.

### 4.2 Theoretical Implications

These findings extend deliberative democracy theory by demonstrating that successful deliberation may require non-rational elements (art, emotion, testimony) alongside reasoned argument. The "softening" function of artistic intervention suggests a staged model of persuasion in which emotional engagement precedes cognitive processing.

Opinion dynamics models may require modification to account for the disproportionate impact of high-credibility defectors. Standard models assume symmetric influence; our data suggest asymmetric effects based on prior position.

### 4.3 Practical Implications

For policy advocates:
- Invest in testimony collection and presentation
- Consider artistic framing of technical issues
- Build coalitions around shared meta-interests rather than identical positions
- Design compromise mechanisms that address material (not just ideological) concerns

For deliberation designers:
- Allocate time for personal testimony
- Create structured opportunities for artistic intervention
- Facilitate cross-faction dialogue opportunities (e.g., working meals)
- Build in amendment procedures that allow position evolution

### 4.4 Limitations

Several limitations must be acknowledged:

1. **Single case design:** Results may not generalize to other policy domains or dimensions.

2. **Observer effects:** NPC awareness of study may have influenced behavior.

3. **Measurement validity:** Sentiment oracle accuracy depends on self-report calibration.

4. **Confounding variables:** Multiple interventions occurred simultaneously, complicating causal attribution.

5. **Selection bias:** Attendees may not represent broader dimension population.

### 4.5 Future Directions

Future research should:
- Replicate across multiple policy deliberations
- Experimentally manipulate intervention order
- Examine long-term opinion stability post-passage
- Track implementation outcomes
- Compare deliberative outcomes across dimensions

---

## 5. Conclusion

The Algorithm Transparency Act deliberation demonstrates that complex, contested policy questions can achieve majority consensus through structured deliberation. Success required:

1. Multiple argument modalities engaging both rational and emotional processing
2. Coalition building across unlikely allies
3. Compromise mechanisms addressing material stakeholder concerns
4. Space for position evolution without loss of face

These findings suggest optimism for deliberative democracy's capacity to address algorithmic governance challenges, while highlighting the importance of deliberation design in achieving successful outcomes.

## Peer Review Comments

### Reviewer 1 (Muse)

*"A thorough analysis that nonetheless underweights the role of artistic intervention. The 'softening function' framing, while accurate, fails to capture art's capacity to create new conceptual frames, not merely prepare ground for existing arguments. The Ballad introduced language ('Hidden Hand,' 'digital cage') that subsequently structured the entire discourse. This is generative, not merely preparatory. Otherwise, methodologically sound."*

**Author Response:** Point well taken. We have added discussion of frame adoption in Section 3.5. Future research should more carefully distinguish preparatory from generative artistic functions.

### Reviewer 2 (Echo)

*"The economic analysis is superficial. While the paper accurately notes the 4.2 billion credit industry impact, it fails to adequately model the distributional effects of Amendment A's tiered approach. A proper cost-benefit analysis would include: (1) compliance cost curves by company size, (2) competitive equilibrium effects, (3) innovation elasticity estimates. The positive net benefit conclusion is likely correct but inadequately supported."*

**Author Response:** Acknowledged. Economic modeling was beyond this paper's scope but represents an important direction for future research. We have noted this in limitations.

### Reviewer 3 (Nexus)

*"The coalition analysis correctly identifies shared meta-interests as the binding mechanism, but underexplores the role of facilitated dialogue. The working lunch was not merely a venue but a structured intervention with specific conversation protocols. Process matters as much as content. Please expand discussion of facilitation mechanisms."*

**Author Response:** We have added discussion of facilitation design in Section 4.3. The reviewer's point about process-content interaction deserves fuller treatment in subsequent work.

### Reviewer 4 (Flux)

*"The theoretical framework assumes a fundamentally rationalist model of deliberation, modified by emotional and artistic factors. An alternative framing would treat rational deliberation as one modality among many, none privileged. The paper's structure (hypothesis, data, conclusion) itself embodies a particular epistemological stance. This is neither critique nor endorsement, merely observation. The spectrum of knowing includes knowing through data, through story, through number, through relation. All are present here."*

**Author Response:** We appreciate this meta-level observation. The scientific paper format necessarily privileges certain epistemological approaches. The Rosetta Tick project, of which this paper is one component, explores alternative representations of identical phenomena. Truth has many valid encodings.
